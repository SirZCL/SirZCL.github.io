      Last year, on Diwali, I was amazed by all of the shining jewels and lights I had encountered. The copious detail in the saris, a garment consisting of a length of cotton or silk draped around the body, and lehengas, a form of skirt which is long, embroidered and pleated, that I had seen so many times, had left me astonished. As I had looked around, the numerous diyas, oil lamps made of clay, seemed to surround me, and had given me warmth on that cold November day. The best part of the celebration, was when my family-friends and I watched fireworks, looked with awe at all who loved us, and reflected back on our lives. Even though I had celebrated this festival innumerable times, last year had seemed to be different, as I had realized how much my ethnic background has affected my life, and will continue to.

      I was born on the sunny June 16, 2003 to two Indian-American citizens; Navjot Kalsi and Puneet Dahiya. My mother had decided to name me Nikita, meaning "Earth" in Russian.Many Indian-American parents tend to name their children traditional Indian names, as they would like their children to remember their culture, and where they came from, but my mom felt that her children didn’t need names to remember what they believe in; she felt that my sister and I should have our own identities. At a young age, I had begun to speak Hindi and English, and learned many Indian prayers that I still say this day. My grandmother, a strong believer in the religion Sikhism, had raised me believing in one god, which has helped me through hard times. My belief in one god has affected my life, as the thought of a greater force and power, watching and guiding me, has allowed me to choose the right choices from wrong.

      My parents, like many other Indian-American citizens, studied and worked hard, so that they could come to America, the "land of dreams". They constantly remind me that I have to work hard, study, and get good grades in order to succeed in life. One of my mother’s mantras, which her father had told her when she was my age, was that one should study for the next ten years, and have fun for the next seventy years, or have fun for the next ten years, and cry for the next seventy years. Unlike most Indian-American parents, my parents decided that it is best to balance studies with fun, and constantly tell me to ‘enjoy my life’ as ‘life is too short’. When I look at my parents, I wish that I could be like them; hardworking, dedicated, and committed, yet through all of the work that they get, they still find time to help my sister and I with whatever we need answers for.

      In March, my family-friends and I celebrate a big Indian celebration called Holi, the celebration of colors. I find that Holi, as well as Diwali, has played a big role in my life. Last year, as I had thrown the sand-colors on everyone, I realized that my culture that I have grown up with, has not been completely Indian, but has also been American. The difference in Holi celebrated in India, and Holi celebrated in America is vast, and I began to see all of the holidays jthat I enjoy, and look forward to, even though they are not ‘my own’. One holiday of this example is Christmas. Every year, as the weather becomes colder, I sit at the edge of my seat, waiting for Christmas to come, and I am the first in my family to decorate the Christmas tree, which has a special place in our house. On Christmas, every year, my parents are the first to wake my sister and me up and give us our presents, which are usually things that I have to wait a long time for.

      As I reflect back on my life, my one question is, ‘who am I?’ The answer to this question, though, is easier to answer than one thinks. The way one would define themselves is by thinking about where they belong, and what they believe in. I would define myself as being an American citizen whose culture in Indian. My ethnic background has shaped the way I think about certain situations, and allows me to be hardworking and dedicated in what I am working on. No matter how much I grow and change in life, I will always hold on to my culture and ethnic background.



      "Before, I did not believe [in Sikhism] and I used to question whether they [the gods] existed, like, 'Where's the proof?'" says fourteen-year-old Rubin Kaur, when asked how her skin condition affected her beliefs. Her skin condition, eczema, is defined as "inflammation of the skin." A couple of years ago it was extremely difficult to live with. Before she had this condition, she was not religious at all and did not believe that there were any "supreme beings." "At one point of my life," she explains, "my eczema was really, really bad, and I felt like my legs were going to be amputated." Her parents prayed and hoped on a daily basis for her to be in better health, but her illness failed to improve. "A couple of months later, [the Sikh prophet] came to visit my friend, and we told him everything." Miraculously, after some religious guidance and reassurance from the prophet, her condition actually improved. "It was mind-blowing, to be honest," Rubin continues, "because I didn't believe, and then that happened, and now I do believe. It's all because of this experience." Now, her entire world revolves around that one event, which allowed Sikhism back into her life.

      Rubin then went on to talk about Sikhism. I asked her if being Sikh, rather than being Hindu, which is the religion of most of the people at our school, affects her. She declined, saying that it "doesn't really affect her life." Besides, Hinduism and Sikhism share lots of principle beliefs. "The concept of Sikhism," she explains, "is being true to yourself and being true to others and being kind." Rubin told me that she had recently looked up Sikhism terms and concepts. The conclusion I drew from that was that she was very interested in her religion and really wanted to know more.

      "We believe in reincarnation, and we take on multiple life forms until our soul becomes pure. Once we become pure, we become one with God," continues Rubin. The concept of Sikhism can also be interpreted in a water cycle metaphor. "First, we are part of the ocean, and then we evaporate into the sky. The condensation part represents life, and we fall as raindrops back into the ocean to join God." When asked for her personal views and opinions on Sikhism, she responded, "I like how we are peaceful people and how our main goal is to be pure, honest and kind."

      Significant figures in her life that helped her grow in her faith are her parents. They are very serious about their Indian and Sikh roots. They constantly remind her of her faith and where she came from. Just like the eczema incident, they've also had a heavy impact on her life. "They're very strict about school," describes Rubin of her parents, "but also want me to have fun and spend time with my family." Her parents also were shaped by their family, and still learn from them even today.

      Rubin often looks back on the eczema incident in her everyday life. The incident made her appreciate life's gifts a lot more. "If it wasn't for that experience, my life would have been completely different." She wouldn't have the beliefs she has today, which would have resulted in a different mindset. "This taught me to live life to the fullest and to appreciate every moment because it was a very painful experience."



      This is a critical analysis essay of Andrew Rosenthal’s opinion article “Beyoncé’s Halftime show Inspires Ridiculous Criticism” which discusses Beyoncé’s halftime show during Super Bowl 50 on Feburary7, 2016. The show sparked criticism from some, including former mayor of New York, Rudy Giuliani, who said that she used the stage to express her political views. The author is expressing his opinion about Mr. Giuliani’s comments. The three main criticisms I had with his essay were: the author is comparing Beyoncé's song and performance to one of the most prominent and courageous acts of protest at the height of the civil rights movement; the author puts words in to Mr. Giuliani’s mouth; and the author leaves a lot to the reader’s imagination.

      As Mr. Rosenthal states, “What do Beyoncé, Tommie Smith and John Carlos have in common? They all managed to get the white political establishment to sputter in outrage by daring to celebrate their identity and mention – gasp – racial politics at a public event.” This implies that Beyoncé was on the same level as John Carlos and Tommie Smith. The two Olympians received great criticism for their courageous act of protest. One also has to keep in mind that the time that the Olympians did this act was in 1968 when the political and social environment was racially charged. It was a time when the country was going through tough times like the assassination of Martin Luther King Jr. Beyoncé singing a song about black empowerment in 2016 when the U.S is more racially united does not compare to the environment in 1968.

      The second criticism is that Mr. Rosenthal extends Rudy Giuliani’s words and also denigrates Mr. Giuliani. For example the author states, “as if the Super Bowl were not entertainment.” He says this in response to Mr. Giuliani who had commented that Beyoncé had to understand that this was the Super Bowl and not Hollywood. Mr. Giuliani didn’t say that the Super Bowl wasn’t entertainment; the author is putting words in his mouth. He downplays the accomplishments of Mr. Giuliani. As he writes in his article, “…former mayor who ran one of the worst presidential campaigns in modern history.” This makes the reader believe that Mr. Giuliani was not well respected. Then Mr. Rosenthal goes on to say that he can barely understand what Mr. Giuliani is trying to say most of the time which further lowers Mr. Giuliani’s status as a politician. Attacking Giuliani doesn’t help the author prove his point because this doesn’t apply to the topic of the article.

      The third criticism is that the author leaves many details in the article to the reader’s imagination. For example when he writes that, “The song is about, among other things, the way the mostly black victims of Hurricane Katrina were and still are ill-served, to put it mildly.” By saying “among many things” he is deemphasizing the main focus of the song and leading one to focus on a small aspect of the song. He is hiding the complete truth from the reader. Another example is when the author says that Beyoncé was attacked on Twitter about the outfits that she and her backup dancers were wearing. This doesn’t tell us if the comments were racist, or if they were just mean; he leaves it open to the reader to interpret this statement.

      I believe that the article by Andrew Rosenthal has not proven the main contention of his article that Mr. Giuliani’s criticism was ridiculous. If one did not know who Rudy Giuliani was prior to reading this article one would have thought that Mr. Giuliani is an unqualified person when really he has accomplished many things in his career as a lawyer, prosecutor, and the mayor of New York City. Also, instead of countering what Mr. Giuliani said Mr. Rosenthal conducts ad hominem attacks against Mr. Giuliani and is vague on the details of the evidence. In coclusion I feel that the author has provided weak evidence and constructed his arguments poorly.

      My grandfather, Harkishin Bhatia, has lived a very interesting life. My paternal grandfather was born on May 7, 1940, seven years before the partition of India. When the partition occured he made the 379 mile journey by bullock cart to his new home in Ajmer, Rajasthan in India. He was born in a lower class family and worked his way up over a career of 40 years. At the age of 75 he has seen a lot of changes in India. From his family moving to different parts of the world, to his main language dissipating as the family tree grows, and the equality of women in India taking place, he has been through it all.

      “We all lived under one roof, my father, mother, me, my wife, son, daughter, three sisters and one brother,” my grandfather said. They lived in a 1000 square foot apartment in the busy streets of the suburb Khar, in the heart of Mumbai, India. “It was cramped with one bathroom, one kitchen, and two bedrooms for a 10 person family.” The family members used to sleep wherever they could, the living room, on the desks, anywhere, but he feels that it was best to have everyone living together so that his brother and sisters could get educated in the city. Also, this was the custom in those days. He has seen that as time has progressed there have been less joint families and more nuclear ones. His entire family spent quality time together and even in their cramped conditions they still had a great bond. Whereas families nowadays are so spread apart that grandparents barely have any relationship with their grandchildren. “Your father and aunt are in the U.S and your grand aunt and uncles are spread out all over the world leaving just me your grandmother; it gets lonely sometimes and I wish I could see you and your brother more,” he revealed sadly.

      Over time, the use of our mother tongue, Sindhi, has been receding. We come from the small village of Nasarpur in Sindh which is in present day Pakistan. In Nasarpur people spoke Sindhi and both my great grandfather and my grandfather knew how to speak, read, and write it. Over time he has noticed that each generation is losing the practice of their mother tongue but advancing in a different language. My great grandfather, in addition to Sindhi, also knew Hindi. The second generation, my grandfather, knows speaks, reads, and writes Hindi, Sindhi, and speaks. The third generation, my father, speaks Sindhi but cannot read or write it, but he can speak and write fluently in Hindi and English. The fourth generation of my brother and I can only understand Sindhi but don’t speak it and we can speak fluent English and Hindi. As he says, “When I was in 1st through 9th the medium of teaching in my school was Sindhi but from 10th through the rest of my career I used English.” English was very valuable to his work; he was a theoretical physicist who worked for the government and spent time in Sweden and England for further training. This job required for him to know English because there was no other means of communication for him.

      My grandfather was a hardworking man and was usually out of the country so my grandmother was running the household. She would clean the house, make the food, and take care of the children; she was a housewife while my grandfather was making the money. My grandfather has witnessed the change in gender equality in India. He has seen that women are coming more into power. Women have been bearing fewer children and working more, which is something that women in his days would not do. My grandfather was one of eight children, and there was no other way to run the family other to have the mother be a housewife. But with fewer children women are getting more educated and gaining more opportunities in the work place. With this taking place he realizes that India is providing more opportunities for women and is changing for the better.

      My grandfather loved that family feel that was provided by having multiple family members living together and he also enjoyed the comfort of having them always with him. He has seen a lot of change as time goes on but believes that change is constant and we should welcome it. He feels this way because he has seen with the gender equality change can be a good thing, and with the language, as we lost one we gained another. So we should appreciate change and evolve with it.

Rather than to not do anything about this issue, solving a piece of this puzzle can save hundreds of lives in the future.

Consider this, since most insurance companies that cover the cost of genome testing only cover it partially, even with insurance coverage it could cost the patient up to 1,000 dollars in out of pocket expenses. Even worse, as I mentioned earlier, some insurance companies don’t even cover genome testing at all, and so for that it would cost the patient anywhere from $2,500 to $5,000, which I find to be absurd. To be frank, neither of the amounts I mentioned in both cases are acceptable. Securing insurance itself is a financially grueling accommodation, and so for people to have to go the extra-mile to look after their well-being is unfathomable to me.

      All in all, science is always advancing, and in this case, it has the capability to provide accurate and crucial medical information to everyone. But there is one financial obstacle that is preventing it from happening, and it is imperative that said obstacle is overcome, in order to secure a happy, healthy future for everyone.

Question: Briefly describe the social and economic conditions of Native American reservations.

Answer: The Native American people began inhabiting the Americas approximately 15,000 years ago. They are believed to originate from Siberia and to have built many civilizations on the land. When people from Europe came to the Americas, they saw the Native Americans as a threat, killed most of them, and had brought disease with them when they came, almost wiping them out. In the present, Native Americans have reservations and are considered one of the poorest races. These reservations have terrible living conditions and bad economic and social statuses. According to the 2010 U.S Census, Approximately 5.2 million Native Americans live on reservations. These reservations have chronic unemployment and immense problems of alcohol use and suicides. There are few instances of economic success; these were mostly when casinos were operated on the lands. Casino developers built casinos on these lands because they do not get taxed by the United States government and are free to do so. Jobs on these reservations are rare and the population there survives because of funds sent from relatives outside of the reservation. The education on these reservations is also very inadequate. For the first part of the 1900’s, children were sent to special boarding schools that were very harsh and forced them to clear their backgrounds and forget any religion or languages. In the 1970’s, reforms gave these reservations more control of the educational system. Many reservations have set up their own school systems, but these schools still don’t have enough funding and resources to be completely adequate. These conditions shouldn’t be tolerated because the Native Americans were one of the first tribes ever to inhabit the Americas. The Europeans tainted the Native American way of life by bringing disease and warfare as well as taking their land, but now in the 21st century it is chronic unemployment and poor education that is impacting the reservations.

Question: What are the pros and cons of “pan-Indianism” from the standpoint of the Native Americans?

Answer: When Columbus first arrived to the new world, he called all of the people there Indians because he thought he had arrived in India. In the 21st century, these people are called Native Americans and they are called First Nations people in Canada. This race consists of many diverse cultures and societies, but some of these people still insist on being called Native Americans as oppose to their specific tribe names. The reason for this is that the Native Americans think that them calling each other all one race gives them more power, this is called “pan-Indianism.” The Native Americans have their reason for doing this, but there are definitely some cons to this practice as well. The Native American tribe consists of many people, but this race has many subdivisions of other races and cultures inside of it that may want to be distinct from others. One Native American tribe may not want to be associated with another. Even with this problem, they still accept the idea of them all being called Native Americans. The reason is that these small tribes alone would not have a lot of power as their own race. When all the tribe populations are combined, it makes up a number that is a good percentage of all of the people in the United States. This gives them more power and say in United States’ government decisions. Many Native Americans do this for political and economic purposes, but still feel that they are separate than others and keep their own traditions. This is definitely a beneficial idea because so many different cultures would not make a huge difference, but one huge culture and race will. Although this is a good decision politically and economically, some of these cultures may not want to do this because they might not want to be associated with other tribes. There are still problems with this, but the Native Americans see this as a good idea and we should respect their decision.
